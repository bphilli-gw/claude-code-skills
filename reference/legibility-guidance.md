# **What are legibility and check-ins?** {#what-are-legibility-and-check-ins?}

### 

## **Legibility**

Legibility means making your work easy for others to understand and disagree with.  
When your research is legible, someone who wasn't involved can quickly grasp:

* What the issue is (the program, grant, or research question)  
* What you think (your bottom line)  
* Why you think it (your key reasons and evidence)  
* How you might be wrong (your main uncertainties)

Legibility is different from transparency. Transparency means making information available. Legibility means making it understandable and contestable. You can publish everything and still be illegible if people can't figure out what you actually think or why.

More on what makes for good legibility [below](#what-makes-for-legible-writing?).

### 

## **Checking in**

Checking in means giving your manager (or yourself) a structured update: your bottom line, why you think it, where you're uncertain, and where you'll go next.

Check-ins help you:

* Clarify your own thinking. Come to a bottom line \- if you stopped now, what would you recommend?  
* Stay aligned with your manager. Make sure you're not going down rabbit holes or missing the point of the investigation.  
* Get input when you're stuck. Surface uncertainties and get help deciding what to do next.  
* Find the right stopping point. Avoid over- or under-investing in a question.

More on what makes for good check-ins [below](#what-makes-for-a-good-check-in?).

## **How they're related**

Checking in and legibility share the same core framework:

* I think X (your bottom line)  
* I think this because Y (key reasons and evidence)  
* I could be wrong because Z (main uncertainties)  
* Next I'll do W (for check-ins: what you'll investigate next)

But more fundamentally, they share the same underlying principle: tell me what you really think. Both require you to be frank, honest, and use plain language. (There’s more on this below.)

You’ll see these principles and structures repeated over and over in our work.

And they feed on each other. If you can check in clearly, your final write-up will be more legible. If you practice legibility, your check-ins will be sharper.

# **What makes for legible writing?** {#what-makes-for-legible-writing?}

Here are some tips on writing legibly, with examples.

## **Main principle: Tell me what you really think**

Don’t worry about a template, don’t include jargon, just tell me what you think in plain language and with honesty about how certain you are (or aren’t).

This is harder than it sounds. Most of us have been trained to write like academics or consultants \- not giving a sharp bottom line, adding flowery language and jargon, and not being clear about ways we could be wrong.

Here’s a bad example related to a question about bednets being used for fishing:

*GiveWell works with the Against Malaria Foundation (AMF) to fund distributions of long-lasting insecticide-treated nets (LLINs) in areas with high malaria burden. LLINs have been shown to be both effective at reducing malaria transmission and cost-effective through multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses demonstrating efficacy in preventing malaria-related morbidity and mortality.*

*However, some researchers and environmental advocates have expressed concern about the potential misuse of insecticide-treated nets for fishing purposes. Evidence from surveys in fishing communities suggests that distributed nets may be repurposed for fishing activities, potentially leading to several negative consequences. These include reduced protection against malaria, depletion of fish stocks due to small mesh sizes capturing juvenile fish, contamination of fish with pyrethroid insecticides, and pollution of aquatic ecosystems from discarded nets.*

*The prevalence and severity of these impacts remain uncertain. Surveys have documented varying rates of ITN fishing across different locations, ranging from minimal usage to over 80% of households in certain fishing communities. If we assume that the practice is relatively rare across most distribution areas and that benefits from malaria protection substantially outweigh potential harms, ITN distributions are likely to remain highly cost-effective interventions.*

Why this is bad:

* What’s the bottom line? How big of a deal do we think this is? How should we adjust our work?  
* What does the writer actually think? This is just a summary of evidence. Is the evidence good? Do we buy it?

Better example:

*A recent news article raised concerns that people in some African countries are using nets for fishing instead of malaria protection, which could harm fish populations and reduce the nets' health benefits.* 

*That’s important because it could mean GiveWell is overestimating benefits of nets by ignoring these negative consequences or missing opportunities to address them.*

*I looked into this, and my best guess is this has almost no impact on our overall work (less than 1% reduction in cost-effectiveness).*

*That's because I think very few people use nets for fishing—maybe 0.1% to 0.5% of people who get a net. Most people don't fish at all, most fishers don't use bednets, and nets are usually destroyed or thrown out when they're no longer protecting against malaria. Even when nets are used for fishing, I think the harm to fish stocks is small (roughly a 2% decrease in yields over 5 years), and fish make up a small share of protein consumption across most of sub-Saharan Africa (under 5%).*

*However, there are probably some rare "hot spot" areas—maybe less than 1-in-200 locations—where bednet fishing is very common (like near Lake Tanganyika, where a survey found 80% of households had fished with bednets). In these spots, I think the reduced net use and fishing harms could cut cost-effectiveness by about 60%. But even with that reduction, these campaigns are still highly cost-effective, and my best guess is they still do much more good than harm.*

*I'm pretty uncertain about the hot spots, though. I'm relying on extrapolations from a few non-representative surveys, mathematical models of fish populations, and conversations with experts—not direct evidence that areas with more bednet fishing actually have worse outcomes. I'm especially uncertain about how long nets are used for malaria protection in hot spots (which drives a lot of the cost-effectiveness reduction) and where exactly these hot spots are, since I'm not sure how to identify them in advance.*

*We plan to keep funding net campaigns, but we're going to ask partners about areas where they've seen bednet fishing and what they're doing to address it. We might also fund research to identify hot spots earlier and test whether different mitigation approaches work.*

Why this is better:

* I have a clear sense of what this write-up is \- it’s responding to a criticism about bednets for fishing.  
* I have a clear sense of what the author thinks. There’s a specific and nuanced bottom line.  
* I know where their conclusions come from and how confident they are.  
* There’s a clear bottom line for our grantmaking \- we’re going to keep funding these campaigns.  
* Use numbers to be clear about what you believe, but still express uncertainty.

Here’s a bad example from a write-up on how concerned we should be about diverting healthcare workers:

*Some academics and policymakers have wondered whether NGOs divert healthcare workers, potentially leading to unintended consequences. A study by Desarrano et al. find that this could lead to negative consequences, potentially even increasing mortality by 10%, suggesting that this could be a concern for charities like those GiveWell supports.*

Why this is bad:

* It’s not specific. What are unintended consequences? Why does diversion matter to GiveWell?  
* What does the writer really think about these? What’s their best guess?  
* What do they think of the evidence? Are there any other considerations beyond this evidence base they’re bringing in?

Here’s a better version:

*When our Top Charities and other programs hire local health workers, are they pulling these workers away from other important health work, offsetting our grants' impact?* 

*My best guess for our Top Charities is that this diversion has minimal impact (\<10% reduction in cost-effectiveness). Intuitively, that’s because campaigns are brief (2-5 weeks annually), deliver what we think are highly cost-effective programs (relative to the average government program), and often don't use existing health workers (or don’t use them in a way that prevents them from completing their other work). This is based on talking to a handful of experts, some light desk research and modeling, and notes from a recent site visit.*

*That said, we identified some cases where diversion could matter more (e.g., we roughly estimate year-round programs requiring scarce government staff where diversion potentially reduces impact by 20-30%). We plan to collect more data on how these programs affect government staff time use and household receipt of non-program healthcare services.*

Why this is better:

* Explains the issue concretely \- the concern is charities hire government workers away from other health programs  
* Gives a clear takeaway \- minimal impact  
* Gives an intuitive rationale for that  
* Explains what evidence they’re drawing on  
* Use numbers to be specific

## **Framework: what you think, why you think it, how you could be wrong**

Use this pattern at every level \- from your top-level summary down to individual sections:

* I think X (your bottom line)  
* I think this because Y (key drivers)  
* I could be wrong because Z (main uncertainties)

A “macro” example:

*See [the summary on the top charities page for SMC](https://www.givewell.org/international/technical/programs/seasonal-malaria-chemoprevention#Summary). We include a bottom line (SMC is highly cost-effective), why we think it (intuitive walk through of cost-effectiveness and other factors), and how we could be wrong (see long list of uncertainties).*

This applies at the “micro” level \- you could imagine clicking into each of the claims in the summary and finding this same structure. An example from the malaria burden section of that report:

*Our estimates of malaria mortality among children targeted for SMC are drawn from the Institute of Health Metrics and Evaluation (IHME) [Global Burden of Disease (GBD)](https://www.healthdata.org/gbd) project. We use 2019 (the most recent published version of GBD at the time of writing) national-level estimates for malaria mortality among children 3-59 months old[111](https://www.givewell.org/international/technical/programs/seasonal-malaria-chemoprevention#footnote111) with the exception of Nigeria, where we use state-level estimates.[112](https://www.givewell.org/international/technical/programs/seasonal-malaria-chemoprevention#footnote112) In Nigeria (but not other locations), we also apply a rough adjustment of \-5 to \-35% (varying by state) to the GBD estimates. This is because we think the GBD estimates might not account for a recent increase in insecticide-treated net campaigns in some states in Nigeria, and could therefore overestimate malaria burden (details in footnote).[113](https://www.givewell.org/international/technical/programs/seasonal-malaria-chemoprevention#footnote113)*

*This results in estimates of annual malaria mortality rates ranging from 0.16% (Togo) to 0.38% (Burkina Faso).[114](https://www.givewell.org/international/technical/programs/seasonal-malaria-chemoprevention#footnote114)*

*These estimates are a source of significant uncertainty in our analysis. Our understanding is that the GBD estimates rely on a number of modeling assumptions,[115](https://www.givewell.org/international/technical/programs/seasonal-malaria-chemoprevention#footnote115) in part because raw data on malaria from health surveillance systems is relatively unreliable in many low-income countries.[116](https://www.givewell.org/international/technical/programs/seasonal-malaria-chemoprevention#footnote116) We have not investigated all the modeling assumptions underlying these estimates in detail and we’re not sure how accurately GBD can attribute deaths to specific causes.*

## **For evidence review: what evidence you found, how you found it, etc.**

When you’re talking about an empirical claim (or probably any claim), you should follow this structure:

* What your bottom line is  
* What evidence you’re using to inform that bottom line and importantly what you think of that evidence (e.g., take it at face value, adjust it downward, etc.)  
* What you’re not sure about  
* What you could do with more time

This is similar to the structure above, but I want to call it out specifically, since a lot of your job will be assessing specific claims.

A good example is from our bednets for fishing report:

*We estimate 25% of people who fish (for subsistence or employment) use ITNs to fish. In a hot spot, we think this number would be 100%. This is a rough guess based on reviewing several surveys and reports of ITN fishing in areas where fishing is common, including two studies from DRC, where we expect to support campaigns conducted over the next 2 years.*

*We did not identify any large-scale, representative surveys of how many fishers use ITNs. Instead, we found two types of sources:*

- *We found six studies estimating the fraction of fishers or people/households using ITNs to fish:*  
  - *Rethink Priorities shared three studies with estimates of the fraction of fishers using mosquito nets based on either household surveys or surveys of fishers, which they reported suggested a prevalence of \~2%, 27% and 66% of fishers using mosquito nets.[21](https://www.givewell.org/international/technical/programs/insecticide-treated-nets/fishing-with-insecticide-treated-nets#footnote21)*  
  - *We also found three additional household surveys in areas of Kenya, Tanzania, and Papua New Guinea where fishing is very common (e.g. coastal areas / fishing villages) that we think suggest percentages of fishers using nets of \~1%, 60%, and \~100%.[22](https://www.givewell.org/international/technical/programs/insecticide-treated-nets/fishing-with-insecticide-treated-nets#footnote22) A simple average of these six estimates gives an estimate that around 40% to 45% of fishers use ITNs.*  
- *We identified three additional surveys which measured the fraction of mosquito nets among fishing gears in areas of DRC and Madagascar, generally finding \~25% of fishing gears involved mosquito nets.[23](https://www.givewell.org/international/technical/programs/insecticide-treated-nets/fishing-with-insecticide-treated-nets#footnote23) While the fraction of gear used for fishing may not perfectly correspond perfectly to the fraction of fishers using these gears (e.g. if fishers are using multiple types of gears, or if some fishers are using many of one type of gear), we’d guess this roughly corresponds to 25% of fishers using ITNs.*  
    
  *If we took an average of the gear based-estimate and the household/fisher-based estimate, we’d estimate around 30% to 35% of fishers use ITNs. We adjust this downward by about 25% to 30% because we think it’s somewhat likely that some studies of ITN focus on areas known to have especially high rates of ITN fishing.*  
    
  *We didn’t review the methodologies of these studies in detail, and we don’t think that we can draw confident inferences about the rates of ITN fishing from them. We are uncertain whether our downward adjustment for sites with higher rates of ITN fishing being especially likely to have been studies/captured in our review is insufficient. We also worry about reporting bias in studies finding especially low estimates of ITN fishing.*  
    
  *We think additional monitoring in areas where fishing is common may help update our beliefs about this number, and we plan to consider funding this.*

## **Additional tips**

* Keep language plain and simple. Don't say "pointed out," say "said." Don't use jargon.  
* Explain what the program actually does \- concretely, not abstractly.  
* Give background on the question you're asking. For example: "We found discrepancies between EvAc and DIL estimates. This write-up investigates why."  
* Use numbers where you can. Instead of "many children," write "15% of children in Chad."  
* Have good citations. Make the work reproducible \- could someone trace your reasoning back to the source?  
* Include a simple, self-contained CEA. Don't bury key assumptions in complex spreadsheets. Show the core math in an intuitive way.  
* Bring in outside-the-model considerations. What do experts think? What are the implementation challenges? Why this charity over others?  
* Be specific about uncertainty. Not just "we're uncertain," but: what you're uncertain about, how big a difference it could make, and what work you could have done but didn't.  
* Pull out the most important things; leave the rest for appendices or footnotes.  
* Legible does not mean deep. If it's a shallow investigation, don't try to give a detailed take. Match the depth of your write-up to the depth of your investigation.  
* Be careful with LLM-generated text. LLMs tend to be:  
  * Overconfident \- they're trained on academic papers and consultant reports, not GiveWell-style writing that emphasizes uncertainty  
  * Overly slick \- polished prose that sounds authoritative but doesn't reflect what you actually think

  Always rewrite LLM output in your own voice, and make sure it reflects your real views and honest uncertainties.

# **What makes for a good check-in?** {#what-makes-for-a-good-check-in?}

A check-in should include:

| Element | Description |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Background** (optional) | What's the program or project? What question are we trying to answer (Skip if your manager already knows, but good to err on the side of your manager knowing less than you think\!) |
| **What I did / found** (optional) | What have you done since last check-in? How has it changed your view? |
| **Current bottom line** | If you stopped now, what would you do? (Yes/no to a grant, rough cost-effectiveness estimate, etc.) |
| **Key uncertainties** | How confident are you? What are you most unsure about? |
| **Next steps** | What are the highest-priority things to investigate, and how long will they take? |

## 

## **When to check in**

* Every few hours of work (more often if you're new to the project)  
* When you're unsure what to do next or feel like you're spinning your wheels  
* At critical decision points (e.g., whether to advance a grant, whether to do a big chunk of additional work)  
* After an important call or information update  
* Before you've even started \- what do you think based on a quick skim?

## **How long should a check-in be?**

* Could be a few bullets, could be a few pages  
* But you should always be able to summarize the key points in a half-page or less  
* Imagine you only have 5 minutes of your manager's time \- what do they need to know?

## **What’s an example?**

This is an example from SMC Adverse Events (which you already did):

* I wanted to share a quick check-in on this question: How much should we be concerned about adverse events from SMC?  
* What I’ve done:  
  * Read the Facebook post  
  * Spent \~30 minutes reading our SMC work  
  * Spend \~1 hour looking for evidence on adverse effects of SP  
  * Did a very rough BOTEC  
* Bottom line: I don’t think adverse events from SMC are likely to offset mortality benefits  
* Why I think that:  
  * We think SMC averts \~3 deaths per 1,000 kids treated. In the literature, I’ve found the upper range of estimates is on the order of \~0.1 per 1,000, and I’m guessing these are overestimated.  
  * Intuitively, it seems like Malaria Consortium or others would’ve raised the alarm about this already.  
* Main uncertainties (in order of priority):  
  * How do they measure adverse events? If they’re undercounted (e.g., screening is weak), then maybe this is a bigger issue.  
  * What about adverse events from AQ? I’ve only been focusing on SP.  
  * Is there literature I missed?  
* Next steps: I’m going to spend \~an hour reading about how adverse events are measured and see if I can find anything about adverse events from AQ. I’m going to leave the others to the side.

# **Your assignment** {#your-assignment}

## **Goal**

Practice checking in and writing legibly through a quick evidence assessment.

This type of shallow investigation is representative of work you'll do at GiveWell \- forming a quick take on whether something is worth deeper investigation.

## **The question**

Should GiveWell prioritize post-discharge malaria chemoprevention (PDMC) for further investigation?

Base your assessment on this recent paper: [Phiri et al., NEJM 2020](https://www.nejm.org/doi/10.1056/NEJMoa2002820)

### 

## **What to do**

*Step 1: Check in before you start (\~15 min)*

Read only the abstract of the paper. Then write a short check-in (half-page max):

* Bottom line: Based on just the abstract, do you think this is something we should fund? Yes, no, or unsure?  
* Why you think that: What's driving your initial take?  
* Key uncertainties: What are you most unsure about?  
* Next steps: What will you look into first?

Send this to me before doing more work.

*Step 2: Investigate (\~1 day of work)*

Dig into the paper and do some light additional research. You might:

* Read the full paper  
* Look at the methods and results in detail  
* Search for other relevant studies or context  
* Think through a rough cost-effectiveness case

Do whatever you think is most likely to update/sharpen your bottom line on this program.

*Step 3: Check in at the \~1 day mark*

Write another check-in (half-page max):

* What I thought before: Recap your initial take  
* What I did: What did you look into?  
* What I think now: Has your view changed? What's your updated bottom line?  
* Key uncertainties: What are you still most unsure about?  
* Next steps: What would you do with more time? Is this worth more investigation?

Send this to me.

*Step 4: Write up your assessment (\~1 day)*

Create a short write-up (under 10 pages) with your take on PDMC. Structure it as:[^1]

1. Summary (1 page max)  
   * Bottom line: Should we prioritize this for further investigation?  
   * Why you think that (intuitive case)  
   * Key uncertainties  
   * What you'd do next with more time  
2. Body (remaining pages)  
   * What is PDMC? (brief background)  
   * Evidence on effectiveness  
   * Rough cost-effectiveness thinking  
   * Other considerations (implementation, scalability, etc.)  
   * Uncertainties and limitations of your analysis

### 

## **What I'm looking for**

* Clear bottom lines at every level \- don't leave me guessing what you think  
* Plain language \- explain things simply  
* Honest uncertainty \- tell me what you don't know and why it matters  
* Match depth to investigation \- not looking for a deep deep dive  
* Specific claims \- use numbers where you can, use citations  
* Good check-ins \- I want to see your thinking evolve

* Check-in \#1: Before you start (send to me)  
* \~1 day of investigation  
* Check-in \#2: At the \~1 day mark (send to me)
