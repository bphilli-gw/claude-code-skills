**GiveWell Style Guide**  
(adapted in September 2023 from earlier [crowdsourced guide](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1mr2OcBVtou3qPRXoNoF8VmWsM0cVkJ9XcY862uFWjyU/edit?usp=sharing))

## How to use this guide

This document is a list of GiveWell style conventions that have been adopted over the years. It is meant to provide general guidance and increase the consistency of published GiveWell materials. It is important that published materials use the correct forms of names and terms identified in the "Required names and terms" below, but there is no need to strictly enforce the rest of the style guide. 

## Required names and terms

**acronyms.** In general, organization names and other phrases that take acronyms should be spelled out the first time you write them in running text (e.g., “Against Malaria Foundation (AMF)”). There is a short list of exceptions to this rule: UN, UNICEF, USAID, USSR, UK, US/USA, IQ, HIV/AIDS, metric units (cm for centimeter, kg for kilogram, etc.), GPS, SIM (card), NGO. If a term will be used only one time in a document, spell it out and do not provide the acronym.

**Against Malaria Foundation (the) / AMF**

**All Grants Fund**

**capitalization**. Headers should be capitalized in sentence-case rather than title case (i.e., capitalize only the first word of the heading, as well as any proper nouns or other words that would normally be capitalized).

**comma.** Use serial (Oxford) comma.  
   
**cost-effective.** Do not abbreviate to "CE" (or anything else) in public-facing documents.

**"developing country"/"developed country."** Avoid "developing country" and "developing countries"; use instead "low-income countries," "low- and middle-income countries" (abbreviated "LMIC"), or middle-income countries. Try to use the term that best fits the settings being discussed, and default to "low- and middle-income countries" or "LMIC" when a generic term is needed. Use "high-income countries" instead of "developed countries." See [here](https://datahelpdesk.worldbank.org/knowledgebase/articles/906519-world-bank-country-and-lending-groups) for a list of World Bank country classifications by income.  

**Dispensers for Safe Water**. Do not shorten to DSW.

**Helen Keller Intl** Shorten the organization's name to "Helen Keller," not "HKI." Use "Helen Keller Intl" (without a final period) not "Helen Keller International" (Also, use updated URL: HelenKellerIntl.org)

**in-line chlorination.** Do not use ILC, and do not capitalize as In-Line Chlorination. 

**insecticide-treated nets** or simply **nets** (not "bednets")

**IRD Global / IRD** Do *not* use Interactive Research and Development.

**"low-income country"/"low- and middle-income countries"/"high-income country."** See the entry above for **"developing country"/"developed country."**

**periods.** Use a single space after periods at the end of a sentence.

**program participants.** Preferred term for people who participate in programs from our recommended charities (rather than "beneficiaries").

**randomized controlled trial (RCT)**

**room for more funding**. Sometimes abbreviated as “RFMF,” but this should be spelled out in text (it may be used in spreadsheets) not be used sporadically in a report, as it is an unfamiliar term and the acronym may therefore confuse readers. 

**Top Charities (and Top Charities Fund)**

**Unrestricted Fund (but unrestricted funding)**

## Other, optional style decisions

Topics are listed in alphabetical order. Some single terms/words are included without explanation; this just means that the way they are rendered here is how we should render them. (E.g., no need to write out a sentence that says we hyphenate "cost-effective"; just include it in the correct form in the list.)

**adverbs and hyphens.** No hyphens needed with adverbs ending in \-ly (e.g., globally acceptable practices).

**American English.** Please use it.

**beneficiaries.** Do not use. See entry for "program participants." 

**British English**. Please change to American English.

**capitalization.**

- Headers and subheaders should use sentence case rather than title case (i.e., capitalize only the first word of the heading, as well as any proper nouns or other words that would normally be capitalized).  
- Source titles should be sentence-case; that is, the first word and any proper nouns should be initial-capped, while all other words should be lowercase.   
    
  If the title has a subtitle that follows a colon, also initial-cap the first word after the colon. If the title consists of two full sentences, treat them as such and initial-cap the first word of each.   
    
  Examples: 

"Are cash transfers made to women spent like other sources of income?"  
"The impact of unconditional cash transfers on nutrition: The South African child support grant"  
"What is the potential of cash transfers to strengthen families affected by HIV and AIDS? A review of the evidence on impacts and key policy debates"

**cluster-RCT**

**comma.** Use serial (Oxford) comma.

**conjunctions.** In formal writing, such as intervention reports and charity reviews, avoid beginning a sentence with a conjunction, such as "and," "but," or "or." It's fine to begin sentences with conjunctions in less formal writing, such as blog posts.

**contractions.** In formal writing, such as intervention reports and charity reviews, we generally avoid contractions ("don't," "can't," wouldn't," etc.). In less formal writing, such as blog posts, donor emails, and donor proposals, contractions are acceptable and useful.

**courtesy titles.** On first mention of a person, use their first and last name. On further mentions, use title (Dr., Professor, Mr., Ms., Mx.) plus last name. The outreach team defaults to using first names.

**dates.** Use “American”-style dates, with the month spelled out before the date and a comma separating the date and year. A comma should also follow the year if the year falls in the middle of a sentence. E.g.: November 2, 1998, was the day I last saw her. (See also **Year ranges**.)

**e.g.**  Place comma after e.g. (e.g., like this).

**em dash.** When setting off a word or phrase, avoid using em dashes when commas will work just as well or arguably better. For instance, "GiveWell looks for great charities, like the Against Malaria Foundation, and great programs" is smoother-flowing than "GiveWell looks for great charities—like the Against Malaria Foundation—and great programs," which unnecessarily emphasizes the text between dashes. 

When using em dashes, no space should be added between them and the adjacent word(s). Do not use en dashes to set off a word or phrase. For example: 

- CORRECT: GiveWell looks for great charities—like the Against Malaria Foundation—and great programs.  
- INCORRECT:   
* GiveWell looks for great charities \- like the Against Malaria Foundation \- and great programs.  
* GiveWell looks for great charities \-- like the Against Malaria Foundation \-- and great programs.  
* GiveWell looks for great charities-like the Against Malaria Foundation-and great programs.

**en dash.** Use an en dash to separate the lead-in word or phrase in a list item from the rest of the list item. For instance, use an en dash when separating the name of a participant in conversation notes from their title. E.g.: 

* **Alan Fenwick** – Director, SCI Foundation  
* **Natalie Crispin** – Senior Research Manager, GiveWell

However, do not use en-dashes for number ranges or other contexts; write out words (to/from) or use a hyphen as needed. 

**estimates.** In running text, use words like "approximately" or "about" instead of "\~" (e.g. "approximately 15,000 LLINs").

**grantmaking**

**i.e.**  Place comma after i.e. (i.e., like this).

**intervention names.** Intervention names (long-lasting insecticide-treated nets, in-line chlorination, etc.) should be lowercased except in specific instances where the name is a trademark (e.g., Sayana Press). 

**lists, numbered, lettered, or bulleted**: 

* There should be parity between list items, so we should avoid lists in which some items are fragments and some aren't.   
* We should capitalize the first word and use final punctuation when the items are full sentences, and do neither when they're not.  
* Lists should be introduced with a colon.  
* NOTE: It's not possible on our website to nest a numbered list inside a bulleted list. 

**lists, in-line**. In-line series may use letters, (a) item a and (b) item b; or numerals, (1) item 1 and (2) item 2\. Note that parentheses should be placed both before and after the letter or number. 

**mathematical symbols**. When writing out a formula in a footnote, use x as the sign for multiplication, \+ for addition, \- for subtraction, / for division, and \= as the equal sign. Please use a space before and after the sign.

**names of organizations.** See "organizational names."

**nonprofit.** No hyphen.

**numbers**. Spell out one through ten; use numerals for numbers larger than ten. If there are numbers under ten and over ten in the same sentence, use numerals for all. For percentages, always use numerals and use the % symbol.

**number ranges**. When used in text, use "to" between the two numbers. For example: 11 to 16 months. 

**organizational names.** In some cases, organizations have particular ways they'd like us to name them, and in other cases GiveWell has a standard approach. These names can be found in this style guide in alphabetical order. Organizations are singular. 

* Wrong: "After the survey results came back, Malaria Consortium decided they would wind down their program in Togo."  
  Right: "After the survey results came back, Malaria Consortium decided it would wind down its program in Togo." 

**page numbers.** Format as "p. \[\#\]" for single-page quotes and "pp. \[\#-\#\]" for multi-page quotes. Page numbers should always follow (and never precede) the source name. Use a lower-case "p" unless it immediately follows the end of the quote with a period. E.g.:

* "This is a quote." Source name, p. 1\.  
* For instance, see Source name: "This is a long, multi-page quote." Pp. 1-2.

**page ranges.** Use hyphens (not en-dashes) in page ranges. Include all of the numerals in each page number.

**percentages.** Always use numerals and use the % symbol.

**program names.** See intervention names.

**quotations, acceptable changes.** Quotations should be rendered as in the original, with the following exceptions: 

* Internal footnote numbers or reference marks should be removed (unless the accompanying note is going to be provided as part of the quotation), but in-text citations should be retained.  
* Single quotation marks may be changed to double, and double to single as needed.    
* The initial letter may be changed to a capital or a lowercase letter as needed to fit the syntax of the sentence.   
* Ellipses are not needed at the beginning or end of a quotation.

**recommended charities**. We are moving away from this phrase. When possible, use "grantees" instead, particularly for organizations that are not top charities. 

**serial comma (Oxford comma).** We use it.

**spelling.** Follow first spelling in Merriam-Webster’s online dictionary.

**titles, courtesy.** See "courtesy titles"

**titles, job.** Capitalize: Dr. Jones, Professor of Epidemiology  
    
**titles of works.** Titles of research papers or journal articles should be enclosed in quotation   
marks. Titles of books and reports should be italicized (without quotation marks) in running text but not in citations. All titles of works should use sentence-style capitalization (see **Capitalization**). 

**WHO**. Use “the” before the full organization name, but not the acronym.

**year ranges.** Year ranges should include all digits: 2021-2023 (not 2021-23). Date ranges in text may use hyphen instead of spelling out "from" and "to.". E.g.: Syphilis screening rates have remained under 20% in 2010-2015.

## Stylistic requirements

GiveWell summary sections (both “In a nutshell”s and sections included under the “Summary” header) should link (more) links that link down to further sections of the report that expand on the summarized points.

## Common references

**CEA disclaimer.** The first time that we reference the cost-effectiveness analysis for a given page, we should include the following disclaimer as a footnote:  
	Note that a) our cost-effectiveness analyses are simplified models that are highly uncertain, and b) our cost-effectiveness threshold for directing funding to particular programs changes periodically. See [GiveWell’s Cost-Effectiveness Analyses webpage](https://www.givewell.org/how-we-work/our-criteria/cost-effectiveness/cost-effectiveness-models#grantmaking) for more information about how we use cost-effectiveness estimates in our grantmaking.

**Temporary x cash disclaimer.** The first time that we refer to a program or intervention’s effectiveness in terms of multiples of cash transfer, the following disclaimer should be included as a footnote:

* To date, GiveWell has used [GiveDirectly](https://www.givewell.org/international/technical/programs/givedirectly-cash-for-poverty-relief-program)'s unconditional cash transfers as a benchmark for comparing the cost-effectiveness of different funding opportunities, which we describe in multiples of "cash” ([more](https://www.givewell.org/how-we-work/our-criteria/cost-effectiveness/cost-effectiveness-models#grantmaking)). In 2024, we re-evaluated the cost-effectiveness of direct cash transfers as implemented by GiveDirectly and we now estimate that their cash program is 3 to 4 times more cost-effective than we’d previously estimated. ([more](https://blog.givewell.org/2024/11/12/re-evaluating-the-impact-of-unconditional-cash-transfers/))  
* For the time being, we continue to use our estimate of the cost-effectiveness of unconditional cash transfers prior to the update to preserve our ability to compare across programs, while we reevaluate the benchmark we want to use to measure and communicate cost-effectiveness.

Note that if the first time we reference a CEA and the first time that we reference x cash are in the same sentence the above two footnote disclaimers should be combined into one combined footnote.

**Moral weights reference.** The first time that we refer to GiveWell’s moral weights, the following footnote should be included:  
	See [this page](https://www.givewell.org/how-we-work/our-criteria/cost-effectiveness/moral-weights) for more on GiveWell’s moral weights.

**Fungibility/leverage reference.** The first time that we refer leverage or funging on a page, the following footnote should be included:  
	See [this page](https://blog.givewell.org/2024/06/21/the-fungibility-question/) for more on how we think about funging and leverage.

**Forecasts reference.** The following note should be included at the beginning of our “Internal forecasts” section  
	For this grant, we are recording the following [forecasts](http://givewell.org/research/internal-forecasts):

## Citation style guide

### Citing sources

Our method for citing sources is designed to make it easy for readers to follow our reasoning and to be simple, so that we don't waste time on arbitrary or unnecessary conventions. 

#### When do I use a footnote? How should it appear?

Anytime a factual statement is made, we should show why we believe it using a footnote. (Footnotes can also be used to add clarification or additional detail that doesn’t fit in the text.) 

**Footnotes should generally include:**

* A quote of the relevant text from the original document.  
  * When referencing a table, image, or something else where a quote cannot be cited, be sure to include enough information for the reader to understand where the information is coming from.  
* A page number for the original document.  
  * If the original document doesn’t have page numbers, use the page as viewed; e.g. if you have a .pdf excerpt of a book, use the book’s page number if possible, but otherwise just use the number of the page within the .pdf.

**Example of a citation with a quote:**

* "In developing countries, particularly in Africa, the vast majority of farmers do not have good access to the local, regional, or global market, and depend on subsistence farming with few advanced inputs." Iimi et al. 2016, p. 2\.

**Example of a citation without a quote:**

* De-Regil et al. 2011, p. 4, table “Summary of Findings for the Main Comparison.”  
* Depending on the information you are cited and how difficult it would be for a reader to identify that information in the cited table, you may choose to provide more information:  
  * De-Regil et al. 2011, p. 4, table “Summary of Findings for the Main Comparison,” row “Anaemia.”

**Formatting guidance:**

* Distinct points in a footnote, e.g. a clarifying point and a citation, should be separated via a bulleted list.  
* In the HTML code itself, put a full empty line after each leading \<fn\> tag, and another before each trailing \</fn\> tag to make the code easier to read. This doesn't affect the way the footnote appears on the webpage. E.g.:

| ...Neither trial found a statistically significant impact (at the 5% level) on diarrhea.\<fn\> "Only four trials used double blinding (Kirchhoff 1985; Austin 1993-i; Austin 1993-ii; Colford 2002), and, significantly, none of them (and hence, none of the pooled estimates thereof) found a statistically significant protective effect from the water quality intervention." Clasen et al. 2009, p. 13\. \</fn\> One possible explanation... |

#### What source nickname should I use?

* **Academic papers:**  
  * If 3 or fewer authors:  
    * \[All authors’ last names\] \[source year (if multiple publication years are given, use print publication year)\]  
    * E.g.: Clasen 2009  
    * E.g.: Clasen, Smith, and McCarthy 2009  
  * If 4 or more authors:  
    * \[First author’s last name\] \[et al.\] \[source year\]  
    * E.g.: Clasen et al. 2009  
    * Note that "et al." takes a period (not "et al")  
  * If the paper is a working paper or preprint, you should indicate this in parentheses in the source nickname.  
    * E.g.: Clasen et al. 2009 (working paper)  
* **Charity documents:**  
  * \[Name of Charity\], \[Title of the document (if the given title is not clear, provide a more descriptive title; use sentence case[^1])\], \[year (if available)\]  
    * E.g.: GiveDirectly, Budget, 2012  
    * E.g.: AMF, Balance sheet, 2012  
  * If a year is included in the title of the document, there is no need to list the year the document was received.  
    * E.g.: Deworm the World, 2019 expenses  
  * You can use an acronym if the organization name is spelled out (with the acronym in parentheses) prior to the first use of the source nickname in footnotes (but note that when multiple source nicknames on a page refer to the same organization, they should all either use an acronym or not use an acronym).  
  * If we have redacted some of the information in the document, please include (redacted) at the end of the source nickname.  
    * E.g.: AMF, Balance sheet, 2012 (redacted)  
* **GiveWell spreadsheet or document:**  
  * \[GiveWell\], \[Title of the document (if the given title is not clear, provide a more descriptive title; use sentence case)\], \[year (of the most recent update)\]  
    * E.g.: GiveWell, Cost-effectiveness analysis of iron fortification, 2019  
    * E.g.: GiveWell, Cost-effectiveness analysis, 2020, Version 1  
    * E.g.: GiveWell, Summary of maternal syphilis diagnostic test adjustments, 2020  
* **Conversation notes:**  
  * For publicly available conversation notes, you should use this exact wording: “GiveWell's non-verbatim summary of a conversation with \[Person\], \[Month Day, Year\]."  
    * E.g.: GiveWell's non-verbatim summary of a conversation with Alan Fenwick and Najwa Al Abdallah, September 14, 2015  
  * If more than 3 people were interviewed, use the organization name.  
    * E.g.: GiveWell's non-verbatim summary of a conversation with SCI Foundation, September 14, 2015  
* **Reports from USAID, WHO, and similar organizations**  
  * When possible (i.e., when authors’ names are provided), follow the convention for academic papers. Otherwise use: \[Organization Name\], \[Title of the document (use sentence case)\], \[year\]  
    * E.g.: World Health Organization, Intermittent Preventative Treatment for Infants Using Sulfadoxine-pyrimethamine (SP-IPTi) for Malaria Control in Africa: An implementation field guide, 2011  
  * If a year is included in the title of the document, there is no need to add the year to the end of the source nickname.  
    * E.g.: World Bank, World Development Report 2008: Agriculture for Development  
  * You can use an acronym in the source nickname for organizations that are well known by their acronym (e.g., UNICEF, USAID, CDC, WHO) or if the organization name is spelled out (with the acronym in parentheses) prior to the first use of the source nickname in footnotes (but note that when multiple source nicknames on a page refer to the same organization, they should all either use an acronym or not use an acronym).  
    * E.g.: UNICEF, Humanitarian Action for Children Overview, 2020  
    * E.g.: According to the World Health Organization (WHO), there are two types of tests used to diagnose syphilis.\<fn\> “Quote.” WHO, The Use of Rapid Syphilis Tests, 2006, p. 24.\</fn\>  
* **Webpages**  
  * \[Author last name (or Organization is author is not listed)\], “\[Page title (use sentence case)\],” \[year (if the page is an article, blog post, or another type of page that is not regularly updated; assuming a publication year is listed)\]  
    * E.g.: World Health Organization, “Micronutrient deficiencies”  
    * E.g.: Devoto, “The hidden costs of fistula repair surgery,” 2018  
* **Database, interactive data tool, etc**  
  * \[Organization\], \[Name of tool (use sentence case)\], \[Short description of the data we looked for (use sentence case)\] (accessed \[Month day, year\])  
    * E.g.: Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation, Global Burden of Disease, GBD Compare, Global trichuriasis prevalence (accessed August 15, 2020\)  
    * E.g.: Joint United Nations Programme on HIV/AIDS, Aids Info, Coverage of pregnant women who receive ARV for PMTCT (accessed October 28, 2020\)  
  * Data from these types of sources are often updated frequently, and a reader of our pages may not be able to find the data we use when replicating a search. We should try to preserve the data that we use from these sources by downloading a file or taking a screenshot. These can be uploaded as the archived version of the source.  
* **Study Records (such as sources from clinicaltrials.gov):**  
  * Clinical Trials Study Record, "Name of trial," YEAR**.**  
    * E.g.: Clinical Trials Study Record, "R21/Matrix-M in African Children Against Clinical Malaria" 2023\.  
* **Informal, unpublished sources**  
  * Note that these items do not need to be included in the “Sources” section of the page.  
  * In the footnote, you should provide enough information for the reader to understand what the source is. Include all of the following (if available): \[Name of informant\], \[Position\], \[Organization\], \[method by which information was received (no capitalization)\], \[Month day\], \[year\] (unpublished)  
  * Bold this information by using \<strong\>\[Source\]\</strong\>.  
  * Examples:  
    * \<strong\>Piali Mukhopadyay, Chief Operating Officer, GiveDirectly, email to GiveWell, January 25, 2012 (unpublished)\</strong\>  
    * \<strong\>Melanie Renshaw, Chief Technical Advisor, African Leaders Malaria Alliance, conversation with GiveWell, November 6, 2019 (unpublished)\</strong\>  
    * \<strong\>Rob Mather, Chief Executive Officer, Against Malaria Foundation, comments on a draft of this page, October 31, 2019 (unpublished)\</strong\>  
    * \<strong\>Jeff Grosz, Accelerator Senior Director and Anna Konstantinova, Accelerator Research Manager, Evidence Action, syphilis case study meeting with GiveWell, January 28, 2020 (unpublished)\</strong\>

For two different sources that would use the same source nickname according to the guidelines above (e.g., two papers published in 2020, one by John Smith and one by Jane Smith), differentiate the sources by adding a letter after the year.

* E.g.: Smith 2020a  
* E.g.: Smith 2020b

If you are updating an existing page that uses different source nickname conventions than those that are outlined here, default to following the existing conventions.

[^1]:  Sentence case: only capitalize the first letter of the first word, as well as any proper nouns (e.g.: To kill a mockingbird)
