General research process recommendations: 

- All GiveWell research is iterative in nature. You should usually aim for conclusions based on shallow research across the problem / program / evidence / cost-effectiveness sections of the QEA before going particularly deep in any one section. It’s also helpful to iterate between desk research and building out BOTECs.There are a few reasons for this iterative process:  
  - Building a model often reveals that certain parameters are going to be very important to the bottom line and thus worth extra scrutiny  
  - Conversely, something you learn in relation to a different question might end up meaning you don’t need to go any deeper on uncertainties from a different section.  
- As a disciplining habit, I (Julie) recommend that you write the particular questions you're going to investigate for each section of the QEA before beginning the research process. After a chunk of work (\~2-4 hours?), write your answers to those questions and any refinement or updates of the questions based on what you've found.   
  - When you come up with a new, more detailed or deeper question, you can ask yourself whether it needs to be answered now or should be set aside for the next stage of research. If you think it should be set aside for later, note that in your write up.  
  - For example: You might set out to define low birth weight and learn that it’s thought to be caused by babies that are small for gestational age (SGA) and by preterm births. After learning this, you should ask yourself whether you need to know more about SGA and preterm birth for this set of research or whether what you know is sufficient and you should move on to other questions.   
- In general, we want to do more work on programs that seem potentially promising and less work on programs that don’t appear likely to have a cost-effective giving opportunity. So in daily work, it’s perfectly fine to e.g. not consider RFMF if you think the program isn’t cost-effective. *Note that during onboarding it’s probably useful to complete the full QEA for training purposes.* 

# What is the problem? {#what-is-the-problem?}

Questions to guide the research process:

- What are the outcomes that I care about? (Usually deaths, morbidity or income / consumption)? How are these outcomes defined and why do they matter?   
- What’s the problem’s prevalence and what population does it affect?   
- Is there disagreement in the field about any of the above (i.e., differing definitions, measurement challenges) and if so what sources am I relying on and why? 

Write up recommendations for “What is the problem?” section

* Give a general statement of the problem that will be addressed by the program.   
  * Link this to the final outcome(s) of interest we use in the CEA. (For example, if it’s an intervention that improves health early in life and we model this as leading to improvements in later-life income, mention this possible link, though caveat appropriately. Examples: complementary feeding, early childhood psychosocial stimulation.)  
* Provide an indication of scale, using trusted sources (e.g., WHO/UNICEF). This should likely tie closely to the “baseline prevalence” used in the CEA.  
* When trusted sources are not available or you’re making a claim that’s more difficult to verify using a simple dataset, especially causal claims (e.g., lack of information is a constraint on adoption of yield-improving farming practices, improvements in health early in life leads to improvements in cognitive skill or adult wages, low birthweight drives high share of neonatal mortality), it’s fine to say that “researchers hypothesize lack of information is a constraint but we haven’t vetted it” or “our impression is that there’s consensus that the first five years are a critical time for brain development, and as a result, interventions early in life may have lasting impacts, but we haven’t reviewed the evidence ourselves or conducted a comprehensive scan of academics’ views on this topic.” Also think about whether these statements are necessary \- if they aren’t, cut them.  
* Don’t add any details that aren’t necessary to achieving the above objectives.

# What is the program? {#what-is-the-program?}

Questions to guide the research process:

- What do we expect to happen in the world through this program? What are the programmatic components?   
- Is the program delivered in a uniform way or is there variation? If there is variation, do I expect this variation to meaningfully influence a program’s effects or cost-effectiveness? 

Specific recommendations for “What is the program?” section:

* Define the program, including key components and target beneficiaries. (Then make sure the “What is its evidence of effectiveness?” section discusses programs with these key components and target beneficiaries.)  
* Describe how the program will address the problem discussed in the “What is the problem?” section.

# Does the program have strong evidence of effectiveness? {#our-process}

Questions to guide research process:

- What is the effect of intervention x on outcome y? And subquestions:   
  - What evidence do I rely on to inform my conclusion? What is the quality of that evidence and what factors drive my assessment of that quality?   
  - What alternative sources of evidence could I rely on and why don’t I rely on them?   
  - If I am synthesizing across different evidence bases, how am I weighting different sources and why?   
- Sometimes relevant (depending on the depth of research):   
  - How plausible is the proposed effect size (e.g., checking causal mechanisms, benchmarking similar interventions etc.)?   
  - Does the intervention have different effects within particular subgroups?   
  - Are there particular variables that appear to moderate the effect? 

Items to include in this discussion:

* Assessment of study quality.  
  * You can use the strength of evidence checklist (RCT: [here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1gJpNgZSW90D0RWj7vZyVhLhf76pIpWCzAd0a8bZpf7Y/edit), meta-analysis [here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1R90o8A39KcltONiGzy1EIk5xtqUnxTTitxMQlZtlptg/edit)) as a guide  
    * Note: You don’t need to formally complete these for all studies, but it provides a good list of factors to review when reviewing studies/meta-analyses  
  * You should describe the important considerations in your assessment of study quality. Are there features that make you concerned about risk of bias? Are there features that give you higher uncertainty? Etc.  
* Basic quality info: type of study, sample size, years conducted  
* Details of the intervention tested   
* Results (including CI)  
* Key characteristics of the study that increase/decrease confidence in its results  
* Mechanism: check it is clear, plausible, supported by the evidence

# How cost-effective is the program? {#our-process}

You should aim to build a model that closely aligns to what we believe the program does in the world and that represents this process as simply as possible. As mentioned above, to do this it often makes sense to iterate between evidence review and building out your model. 

A common failure mode is building models that are too complex and losing sight of the most important assumptions we’re making. So as you’re building your model, ask yourself: “Do I understand the model? Which are the most sensitive parameters? Is that intuitive? Have I prioritized looking into the parameters that make the biggest difference to the bottom line?”

# Is there room for more funding? {#our-process}

* What is the likely gap in funding? Are there other major funders that are active in this space (and if so should we be concerned about funging)?   
  * Need based approach: How many people have the relevant condition and aren’t being treated? What is a reasonable portion of those people a program could treat? What are the approximate costs of treatment?  
  * Specific implementer based approach: Have we heard about particular funding gaps from implementers?
